Why We're Here

Harvard Maintenance has operated on Atlassian Service Management for five years, and the core problem hasn't changed: every IT request still routes to a human. With 10,000-12,000 monthly inquiries handled by 22 IT staff (six on the service desk), and 1-3 acquisitions per year adding roughly 500 employees each time, scaling headcount proportionally is no longer viable. Travis has seen peers deflecting 75% of tickets autonomously and can't even measure Harvard Maintenance's own baseline. That gap is what this evaluation is designed to close.

We are jointly evaluating Fin AI as the front door for Harvard Maintenance's IT support - handling high-volume, repeatable requests (password resets, account unlocks, onboarding access, hardware lookups) autonomously while human agents focus on complex work that requires judgment. The pilot begins with password resets as a contained, high-impact proof point, then expands to broader IT workflows. The goal is a clear, data-backed answer: can Fin resolve a meaningful share of Harvard Maintenance's ticket volume without compromising the experience employees expect?

What We Keep Hearing

Everything still routes to a human

Harvard Maintenance has been on Atlassian JSM for five years and the gap is clear: their AI strategy empowers agents rather than deflecting work away from them. A peer organisation is deflecting 75% of tickets autonomously. Harvard Maintenance can't measure its own baseline yet - and that's exactly what this evaluation is designed to change.

Acquisitions make headcount unsustainable

1-3 acquisitions per year at roughly 500 employees each creates a predictable IT volume surge the current model can't absorb without proportional hiring. The team needs Fin to absorb that growth so headcount stays flat.

Password resets are the right starting point

High volume, well-understood process, involves InfoSec requirements that test Fin's human-in-the-loop capability. The team agreed this is the right proof point - contained, measurable, and representative of broader automation potential.

Microsoft Teams is a critical dependency

Harvard Maintenance is moving the entire company to Teams. Travis wants employees to initiate IT support from a single Teams button. This integration is on Intercom's roadmap and is a material dependency for company-wide rollout.

Knowledge base needs curation before Fin can use it

The IT knowledge base is split across SharePoint and Confluence with a lot of undocumented, agent-held knowledge that hasn't made it into writing yet. The team prefers a selective, API-based import approach over bulk sync - bringing in only the articles that are accurate and relevant before training Fin on them.

Change management is the real risk - not technical integration

Travis said it plainly: internal change management is the biggest challenge for rollout, not the technical setup. Q3 (July-September) is the target, giving time to prepare the organization to shift away from human-routed requests.

Your Perspective

What's Resonated

  • Outcome-based pricing - only paying when Fin resolves without human intervention; Travis called this out in the first call
  • Fully integrated helpdesk + AI + human-in-the-loop - not a bolt-on AI layer over a broken ticketing system
  • API connectors for live employee data - hardware lookups, software access checks, device info by employee email
  • Speed of setup - password reset with 1-2 API endpoints estimated at two weeks; "days not months" resonated
  • Reporting depth - resolution rates, sentiment, topic spikes, content and action gap recommendations
  • Intercom uses Fin internally for IT support - Travis flagged this as meaningful
  • Rafael called Fin an "employee" - the team started thinking about Fin as a team member, not a ticket deflector
  • MCP server support - eliminates manual data connector creation for environments with existing MCP infrastructure

What We're Working Through

  • Microsoft Teams integration - not yet available; needed for company-wide rollout beyond the IT team's use of Slack
  • Pricing at scale - how the per-resolution model works as ticket volume grows; we've walked through options to size this accurately
  • Security review - Intercom needs to be whitelisted in Harvard Maintenance's security filtering; SOC 2 review underway
  • Password reset automation - the workflow touches InfoSec-owned infrastructure; the security team needs to sign off on the final automated step
  • Knowledge base readiness - a content review and selective import is needed before Fin can answer accurately at scale

The End State

By the end of this evaluation, Harvard Maintenance's service desk team is spending meaningful time on complex, judgment-required work - not triaging password resets and account unlocks. The six people on the service desk aren't answering the same questions for the fiftieth time this month. Fin handles the volume; they handle the problems that actually need a human.

For IT leadership, the acquisition story changes. When the next 500 employees come onboard from a new acquisition, there's no accompanying spike in ticket volume that requires new hires. Fin absorbs it. The knowledge base improves with each interaction - Fin surfaces content gaps, Rafael and Sean review them, and the system gets smarter. Travis has a deflection rate he can report upward, alongside CSAT scores and resolution time data that didn't exist under Jira.

For employees across Harvard Maintenance, IT support is faster and available at any hour. A password reset at 11 PM doesn't wait until morning. A new hire on their first day gets through onboarding without opening a ticket that sits in a queue. The interaction feels like talking to someone who knows their system - because Fin has access to the same employee data the service desk uses.

The Business Case

Current state: 10,000-12,000 IT support requests per month, handled by a 6-person service desk. Every ticket touches a human. Industry benchmarks put internal IT ticket cost at $15-20 per ticket when accounting for fully-loaded agent time. At a conservative $15/ticket, Harvard Maintenance's current model costs roughly $1.8M-$2.2M annually in IT support labor.

With Fin at 68% resolution: Approximately 6,800-8,160 tickets per month resolve autonomously - password resets, account unlocks, onboarding access, hardware lookups, VPN issues. Human agents handle the remaining 30-35% of complex requests that genuinely benefit from their expertise.
